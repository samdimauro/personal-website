Intro

I've spent most of my twenties in small rooms with big sound. The kind of nights where you spot a flyer on Instagram, confirm a rumour in a group chat, jump to Resident Advisor for details, then to SoundCloud to decide if the set is your thing. That pinball-machine journey is normal in underground music. It's also exhausting. With Kigs, we set out to make discovery feel like the culture actually works — social first, trust-led, music forward.

What

Early-stage social product for underground music discovery, event planning, and crew coordination.

Why

Discovery is social by nature — but the tools people use are fragmented and trust-blind.

The problem

Too many apps, not enough trust.

Finding a good night out in underground music is a multi-app, multi-tab, multi-group-chat exercise. You hear about something through a friend, confirm it on Instagram, research the lineup on Resident Advisor, audition the sound on SoundCloud, then coordinate with your crew on WhatsApp. None of these tools talk to each other. None of them know your taste or your circle.

The drop-off between discovering a night and actually going is enormous — not because people aren't interested, but because the friction is real. Existing platforms either cast too wide a net or are too passive. None centre the social layer that actually drives decisions in underground music: who's going.

We interviewed 5–8 underground music fans across London, Toulouse, Sydney, and California — mapping real discovery and planning behaviours into jobs-to-be-done — then ran a paid JTBD survey (~200 respondents). Three patterns kept repeating:

•

Social trust beats algorithms.

If someone I trust is going, the event earns my time.

•

Discovery is fragmented; curation shouldn't be.

Surprise me, but keep the quality bar high.

•

Planning is the real friction.

Finding something good is step one. Coordinating the crew is the headache.

Design

Starting with objects, not screens.

Rather than jumping to UI, I mapped the ecosystem using an object-based mindset — users, events, artists, venues, mixes — agreeing the backbone fast before touching screens. The mental model: discovery begins with events and people, supported by music. Not the other way around.

Navigation took three meaningful iterations. We needed a structure that matched how people actually think about going out — entry through events, social signals surfaced early, and the ability to explore without losing context.

V1

Everything unified into a home with carousels. Looked clean but blurred intent — too much happening at once.

V2

Feeds separated from Events and Map. Better mental model, but home felt thin and underworked.

V3

Events as the entry point, friend-powered signals early, Search decoupled from Map. The right balance.

Event as a post-like object

Treating events as social artefacts — likeable, commentable, repostable — unlocked familiar behaviours without building a heavy social network from scratch. It matches how people actually share nights: not as transactions, but as things worth telling someone about.

A map with social proof

Progressive disclosure keeps it legible — light clusters zoomed out, attendance badges and date chips appearing as you zoom in. You're not just picking a venue; you're joining a night with your people.

Launch

Taking it to Toulouse.

To stress-test the product with real people, we hosted a launch event in Toulouse — a club night with DJs — and listed and promoted it entirely inside Kigs. It gave us first-hand feedback on trust signals, planning flow, and map legibility alongside early usage data to compare against prototype learnings.

Early signals confirmed our instincts: people prefer event-first entry, friend and promoter trust changes which events they open, and a gentle planning layer is enough to move nights forward.

What's next

Two honest bets.

As live data grows, we'll tune around trust signals rather than a heavy recommendation engine. Both bets are measurable and both stay true to the trust-first approach that defines the product.

•

Second-degree attendance.

Surfacing who-of-who is going should lift event taps without needing a recommendation algorithm.

•

Events ↔ artists ↔ mixes.

Connecting these three objects into a single follow-through path should increase depth of engagement — and reduce the app-hopping Kigs was built to solve.

The app is live, the dataset is embarrassingly small, and three engineers are already disagreeing about what to build next. Honestly, that last part is a good sign.
